Survival of the fittest

June 17, 2025
⦿

Navigating a World That Won’t Stand Still #

In early 2019, we lived in a different world. It felt slower, the horizons were predictable, and our niches seemed stable. Ironically, while the world was calm, I was the one chasing uncertainty—eager to expand my territory and bring order to chaos by defining clear boundaries and systems. It was the start of a journey that took me from the comfortable certainty of variable naming and cache invalidation into a messy, uncharted territory of entropy and unknowns.

The Spark: A Late Night in February #

I was leading a personalization project with a talented team. We had ambitious goals, and my calendar was overflowing. One cold, windy evening in February, the office had mostly cleared out. I was tucked away in our team’s corner, typing up meeting notes on my laptop. In that quiet moment—free from the daily noise and without my usual noise-canceling headphones—my mind started to wander. I found myself thinking about nature. specifically, about emergence. I remembered reading about periodic cicadas and bird murmurations. These aren’t managed by a “boss” bird or a “manager” insect. Yet, the cicadas synchronize perfectly, and the birds form complex, shifting clouds to defend themselves. They don’t have a master plan; they have context. They understand their immediate surroundings and neighbors, and from that, survival strategies emerge. I sat there wondering: If insects and birds can learn to navigate complex environments just by reacting to their context, why can’t we? That question changed everything for me. It pushed me to look beyond the code and seeing the intricate connections between our data, our teams, and our strategy. It forced me to realize that in 2024, the “context” for doing tech is far less predictable than it was in 2019. We can’t just build rigid systems anymore; we need resilient ones.

Value Creation is About Killing Uncertainty #

In business, we often talk about “value” as if it’s a static thing we manufacture. But fundamentally, value creation is about minimizing uncertainty. When you reduce the unknowns in your operations or product-market fit, you create value. But here is the catch: you can’t program this linearly. Value flows like a living thing. It emerges from a million interconnected processes—product development, customer feedback, supply chain shifts. You can’t perfectly control it. It’s an ecosystem.

The Trap of Automation #

We often try to tame this ecosystem with automation. We think, “If we automate the deployment cycle, we’ll get predictable value faster.” And yes, automation speeds things up. But it’s not free. As we automate, we hide complexity under the rug. Suddenly, you need specialized roles—DevOps engineers, cloud architects—just to understand the machine you built. You end up with a “coordination problem.” Every piece of new tech requires more people to talk to each other to keep it running. We end up scaling our problems instead of solving them.

Shifting from Scale to Scope #

This brings us to the core shift we need to make: From Economy of Scale to Economy of Scope.

  • Economy of Scale is about doing the same thing faster and cheaper. It works great until the environment changes.
  • Economy of Scope is about learning. It’s about having a team that understands the context so well that they can pivot when the world shifts. If we only focus on scale, we burn out. We get “allostatic overload”—organizational exhaustion. We build rigid structures that snap when the wind blows.

Designing for Agency #

So, how do we survive? We look back to the birds. We need to design for agency. We can’t control the complexity of the market from the top down. Instead, we need to create conditions where teams (agents) have the power and context to make decisions on the ground. We need to:

  1. Distribute the load: Don’t let one part of the system bear all the strain.
  2. Create boundaries: Give teams a safe “niche” where they can reduce uncertainty and get work done.
  3. Embrace the timeline: Treat our products and data as “temporal objects”—things that evolve over time.

Conclusion #

Fitness isn’t about being perfect; it’s about adapting. The path to survival in this hyper-fast world isn’t through tighter control or more blind automation. It’s through fostering agency. It is about building organizations that, like the murmurations of birds, are fluid, responsive, and alive. We need to stop building machines and start nurturing organisms.
